How to Read Your Eye Prescription

Shree Hari Chasma Ghar 10 Dec, 2025 6 min read

You have just had your eyes tested, and the optician hands you a little card covered in abbreviations and numbers — SPH, CYL, AXIS, ADD, OD, OS. To most people it looks like a secret code. The truth is that an eye prescription is simply a precise set of instructions for making your lenses, and once you know what each column means, it becomes easy to read. This guide decodes your prescription piece by piece so you can understand exactly what your own vision numbers are telling you.

OD and OS: Your Two Eyes

Prescriptions are laid out in rows, one for each eye. You will usually see OD and OS, which come from Latin — oculus dexter for the right eye and oculus sinister for the left. Some opticians simply write R and L instead. Each eye is measured separately because they very often need different corrections, so always read the two rows independently.

SPH (Sphere): The Main Power

The SPH, or sphere, is the core of your prescription — the main lens power that corrects short-sightedness or long-sightedness. The sign in front of the number tells you which:

  • A minus sign (−) means you are short-sighted (myopic) and struggle to see distant objects clearly.
  • A plus sign (+) means you are long-sighted (hyperopic) and close objects are harder to focus on.
  • The further the number from zero, the stronger the correction — for example, −4.00 is stronger than −1.00.

CYL and AXIS: Correcting Astigmatism

If your prescription has values under CYL and AXIS, you have some astigmatism — a very common condition where the eye's surface is shaped a little more like a rugby ball than a football, causing slightly blurred or stretched vision. These two figures work as a pair:

  • CYL (cylinder): the amount of extra power needed to correct the astigmatism.
  • AXIS: a number between 1 and 180 that tells the lab the exact angle at which that cylinder power must sit.

If these columns are blank, it simply means you have little or no astigmatism — which is perfectly normal.

Quick Tip

Astigmatism is extremely common and nothing to worry about — it is just a shape variation, not a disease. If you see numbers under CYL and AXIS, it only means your lenses will be made to bring that angle into sharp focus.

ADD: Help for Reading

The ADD value is an additional plus power placed in the lower part of the lens to help you see up close. It appears when you need reading, bifocal or progressive lenses — most commonly from your forties onward as presbyopia sets in. The same ADD figure usually applies to both eyes, and it is what allows a single pair of progressive glasses to handle both distance and near vision.

PD: Pupillary Distance

You may also see PD, or pupillary distance — the gap between the centres of your two pupils, measured in millimetres. This ensures the optical centre of each lens lines up perfectly with your eyes, which is essential for clear, comfortable vision, especially in stronger prescriptions and progressive lenses. Accurate PD measurement is one big reason getting glasses fitted in person beats guessing online.

Reading a Prescription in Order

Put it all together and reading your card becomes straightforward. Work through it like this:

  1. Identify the two rows — right eye (OD/R) and left eye (OS/L).
  2. Read the SPH to see the main power and whether it is minus or plus.
  3. Check CYL and AXIS to see if astigmatism is being corrected.
  4. Look for an ADD value if you need help reading up close.
  5. Note the PD if it is listed, for accurate lens centring.

That is genuinely all there is to it — a set of clear instructions, not a mystery.

FAQs

Common questions about reading an eye prescription

What do SPH, CYL and AXIS mean on my prescription?

SPH (sphere) is the main lens power that corrects short or long sight, with a minus sign for short-sight and plus for long-sight. CYL (cylinder) is the extra power that corrects astigmatism. AXIS, a number from 1 to 180, tells the lab the angle at which that cylinder power should sit.

What does ADD mean on an eye prescription?

ADD is the additional plus power added to the lower part of the lens to help you see up close. It appears when you need reading, bifocal or progressive lenses, usually from your 40s onward as presbyopia develops.

What do OD and OS stand for?

OD stands for the Latin oculus dexter, meaning your right eye, and OS stands for oculus sinister, meaning your left eye. Some prescriptions simply write R and L. Each eye can have different values, so the two rows are read separately.

Is a higher prescription number worse?

A number further from zero, whether plus or minus, means a stronger prescription and thicker lenses, but it does not mean your eyes are unhealthy. It simply describes how much correction you need. Only a full eye examination can assess eye health.
