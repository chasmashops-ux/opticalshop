Common Eye Problems and Their Solutions

Shree Hari Chasma Ghar 22 Mar, 2026 7 min read

Almost everyone runs into an eye niggle at some point — blur that will not clear, gritty dryness after a long day at the screen, or eyes that turn red and watery in the dust. Most of these everyday problems are common, understandable and manageable. Knowing what is behind them helps you take the right small step and, just as importantly, recognise the moments when you should see a professional. Here is a plain-English tour of the eye problems we hear about most, and what actually helps.

Refractive Errors: Blurry Vision

By far the most common reason people visit an optical shop is simple blur — the eye not focusing light correctly. These "refractive errors" come in a few forms:

  • Myopia (short-sightedness): distant objects look blurry while near ones stay clear.
  • Hyperopia (long-sightedness): near objects are harder to focus on.
  • Astigmatism: an unevenly shaped cornea blurs both near and far vision.
  • Presbyopia: the natural, age-related difficulty reading small print after about 40.

The solution is usually straightforward: an accurate eye test followed by the right glasses or contact lenses. These are corrections, not diseases, and modern lenses handle them very comfortably.

Digital Eye Strain

Hours of phones, laptops and TV leave many people with tired, aching eyes, blurred vision and headaches by evening. This is digital eye strain, and it comes from long, unbroken focusing and reduced blinking. The fixes are simple and effective: take frequent breaks, follow the 20-20-20 rule, keep screens at a comfortable distance, and consider blue cut lenses with an anti-glare coating for heavy screen days.

Dry Eyes

Dry eye leaves the surface feeling gritty, scratchy or strangely watery. It is common in air-conditioned offices, dusty conditions and among frequent screen users who forget to blink. Staying hydrated, blinking consciously, reducing direct fan and AC airflow, and using lubricating eye drops when needed usually bring relief. If dryness is persistent, an eye-care professional can look for the underlying cause.

Quick Tip

Blink on purpose during screen time. We blink far less when concentrating on a screen, which dries the eyes. Every few minutes, take a couple of slow, full blinks and glance away into the distance — it re-spreads your tear film and eases that tired, gritty feeling.

Red, Itchy or Watery Eyes

Redness, itching and watering are usually the eye reacting to something — dust, pollution, smoke, allergies or tiredness. Ahmedabad's dust and seasonal changes make this especially common. Gentle measures help: rinse with clean water, avoid rubbing, rest the eyes and stay away from known triggers. Most mild cases settle on their own. But if redness comes with pain, discharge, strong light sensitivity or lasts several days, it is time to get it checked rather than keep self-treating.

Eye Infections and Irritation

Infections such as conjunctivitis (often called "pink eye") can cause redness, stickiness and discomfort, and some spread easily from person to person. Good hygiene is your best defence — wash hands often, avoid touching or rubbing the eyes, and never share towels or eye cosmetics. Infections generally need proper professional assessment, so avoid random over-the-counter remedies and see a qualified practitioner for the right treatment.

Age-Related Concerns

As we get older, other conditions become more relevant, such as cataract (a clouding of the lens) and other changes that a professional monitors over time. These are not things to diagnose or worry about at home, but they are a strong reason to keep up regular eye tests as you age, so any change is spotted early when it is easiest to manage.

Simple Habits That Protect Your Eyes

Whatever your age, a few consistent habits keep most everyday problems at bay:

  1. Get a routine eye test, even when your vision feels fine.
  2. Take regular screen breaks and rest your eyes through the day.
  3. Wear UV-protective sunglasses in bright sunlight.
  4. Eat a balanced diet with plenty of fruit and vegetables, and stay hydrated.
  5. Keep your hands and eyes clean, and avoid rubbing them.

When to See a Professional

Reach out promptly for sudden vision loss, eye pain, an injury, persistent redness or discharge, or new flashes and floaters — these need timely attention. For gentler, ongoing issues like blur, headaches or mild irritation, a routine eye test is the sensible starting point. FAQs

Common questions about eye problems

What are the most common eye problems?

The most common issues are refractive errors like short-sightedness, long-sightedness and astigmatism, along with digital eye strain, dry eyes, watery eyes, redness and mild irritation. Most of these are manageable with the right glasses, simple habits or basic care, but a proper eye test confirms the cause.

Why do my eyes feel dry and tired after screen use?

Staring at screens reduces how often you blink, so the tear film dries out and eyes feel gritty and tired. Taking regular breaks, blinking consciously, adjusting screen brightness and using lubricating drops when needed usually helps. Blue cut lenses with anti-glare can add comfort for heavy screen users.

Are red or watery eyes serious?

Occasional redness or watering from dust, tiredness or allergies is common and often settles on its own. However, if redness, pain, discharge or watering is severe, lasts several days or affects your vision, it is important to see an eye-care professional promptly rather than self-treating.

When should I see a professional about an eye problem?

See a professional if you have sudden vision loss, eye pain, persistent redness, flashes or floaters, an injury, or blur that does not clear. For general blur, headaches or ongoing discomfort, a routine eye test is the sensible first step to find out what is going on.
