Myopia: Causes and Prevention

Shree Hari Chasma Ghar 20 Apr, 2026 7 min read

More and more children are squinting at classroom boards, and more adults are reaching for glasses to read a signboard down the street. The reason is myopia — short-sightedness — and it is becoming remarkably common across India and the world. The good news is that while myopia often cannot be fully avoided, understanding what drives it and building a few sensible habits can genuinely help slow how fast it grows, especially in young eyes. Here is a clear, practical look at causes and prevention.

What Is Myopia?

Myopia means you can see close-up objects clearly, but things in the distance appear blurry. It usually develops because the eyeball grows a little too long from front to back. Light entering the eye then focuses just in front of the retina rather than directly on it, so distant images lose their sharpness. Glasses, contact lenses or certain lens designs shift that focus back onto the retina and restore clear vision.

Common Signs to Watch For

Myopia often creeps in quietly, particularly in children who may not realise their vision is not normal. Keep an eye out for these clues:

  • Sitting very close to the television or holding books and phones near the face.
  • Squinting or screwing up the eyes to see distant objects.
  • Trouble reading the board at school or road signs while travelling.
  • Frequent headaches or tired, rubbing eyes after visual tasks.
  • Complaints of blurry distance vision or losing interest in outdoor games.

What Causes Myopia?

Myopia is driven by a mix of factors, and rarely just one:

  • Genetics: if one or both parents are short-sighted, a child is more likely to develop it too.
  • Prolonged near work: long hours of reading, studying and screen use put constant strain on the focusing system.
  • Too little outdoor time: reduced exposure to natural daylight is strongly associated with faster progression in children.
  • Poor lighting and posture: reading in dim light or with the material too close adds to the load.

These factors combine differently for each person, which is why two children in the same class can have very different vision.

Quick Tip

Encourage the "outdoor hour." Aim for at least an hour of outdoor daylight play every day for children. Time spent in natural light is one of the simplest, most researched habits linked to slower myopia progression in growing eyes.

Can Myopia Be Prevented?

Let us be honest: when myopia runs strongly in the family, it may not be preventable altogether. But its speed can often be slowed, and that matters — a lower final prescription is easier to live with and better for long-term eye health. Prevention is really about managing how much strain young eyes take on and how much daylight they get.

Practical Steps to Slow It Down

These everyday habits support healthier vision, especially for children whose eyes are still developing:

  1. Prioritise outdoor time: aim for daily time outside in natural light.
  2. Follow the 20-20-20 rule: every 20 minutes of near work, look at something about 20 feet away for 20 seconds.
  3. Keep a healthy distance: hold books and screens at a comfortable arm's-length rather than right up to the face.
  4. Light the room well: read and study in bright, even light, never in the dark with only a screen glowing.
  5. Balance screen time: break up long stretches of phones and tablets with other activities.
  6. Get regular eye tests: catch changes early so glasses stay accurate.

Myopia Control Options

Beyond everyday habits, there are dedicated approaches an eye-care professional may discuss for children whose myopia is increasing quickly, such as specialised spectacle lenses, particular contact lens designs, or other clinically supervised methods. These are decisions to make with a qualified professional after a proper examination — not something to try on your own. The right choice depends on the child's age, prescription and how fast it is changing.

When to See a Professional

Any time vision seems blurry, is changing, or a child shows the signs above, book an eye test. Existing glasses wearers with myopia usually benefit from a check at least once a year, since the prescription can shift as children grow. A proper test is the only reliable way to measure myopia and rule out other causes of blur.

FAQs

Common questions about myopia

What is myopia in simple terms?

Myopia, or short-sightedness, means you can see nearby objects clearly but distant ones look blurry. It usually happens because the eyeball grows slightly too long, so light focuses just in front of the retina instead of directly on it. Glasses or contact lenses correct the focus.

Can myopia be prevented completely?

Myopia cannot always be prevented, especially when it runs in the family, but its progression can often be slowed. Plenty of outdoor daylight, regular breaks from close work and good lighting all help, particularly in children whose eyes are still developing.

Does too much screen time cause myopia in children?

Extended near work such as phones, tablets and reading is linked to faster myopia progression, and reduced outdoor time makes it worse. Screens are not the only cause, but balancing close work with daily outdoor play is one of the most helpful habits for growing eyes.

How often should a short-sighted child have an eye test?

Children with myopia should usually be checked at least once a year, or sooner if vision seems to change, because their prescription can shift quickly as they grow. Regular tests catch increases early so glasses stay accurate and any concerns are picked up in time.
