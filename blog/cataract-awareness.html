Cataract: Symptoms, Causes and Awareness

Shree Hari Chasma Ghar 26 Mar, 2026 7 min read

Cataract is one of the most common reasons vision becomes cloudy as we get older — and one of the most misunderstood. Many people imagine it as a "film" growing over the eye that must be scraped away. In reality it is a slow, natural change inside the eye, and being aware of the early signs makes a real difference. This guide explains what a cataract is, the symptoms to look out for, what raises your risk, and why regular eye tests matter. It is general information, not a diagnosis, so always speak to an eye-care professional about your own eyes.

What Exactly Is a Cataract?

Inside your eye, just behind the pupil, is a clear natural lens that focuses light onto the back of the eye so you see a sharp image. A cataract is simply a clouding of that lens. As it becomes less transparent, light cannot pass through as cleanly, and vision gradually turns hazy, dim or blurred — a bit like looking through a foggy or smudged window. It usually develops slowly over months or years, which is why many people do not notice it at first.

Common Symptoms to Recognise

Cataract symptoms come on gradually and can be easy to dismiss as ordinary ageing. Watch for a combination of these:

  • Cloudy or blurry vision that a new pair of glasses does not fully fix.
  • Increased glare and sensitivity to bright sunlight or lights.
  • Halos around lights, especially headlights while driving at night.
  • Faded or yellowed colours that look less vivid than they used to.
  • Needing brighter light to read or do close work.
  • Frequent changes in your glasses prescription.

Only a proper eye examination can confirm whether these signs are due to a cataract or something else, so do not self-diagnose.

Quick Tip

If your night driving has become noticeably harder — more glare, more halos around headlights — do not just accept it as part of getting older. It is a common early clue for cataract and other eye changes, and a simple eye test can find out what is going on.

What Causes Cataracts?

By far the most common cause is simply age — the lens naturally changes over a lifetime. But several other factors can play a part in how early or quickly a cataract develops:

  • Ageing: the leading reason, common in older adults.
  • Long-term UV exposure from the sun without eye protection.
  • Diabetes and other health conditions.
  • Smoking and heavy alcohol use.
  • Eye injury or previous eye surgery.
  • Long-term use of certain medications as advised by a doctor.

Can You Lower Your Risk?

There is no guaranteed way to prevent cataract, but a few sensible habits are good for your eyes and general health, and may help:

  1. Wear sunglasses with proper UV protection when you are out in bright sun.
  2. Avoid smoking and limit alcohol.
  3. Eat a balanced diet rich in fruit, leafy greens and colourful vegetables.
  4. Keep conditions like diabetes well managed with your doctor's guidance.
  5. Have regular eye tests so any changes are noticed early.

When to See an Eye Specialist

You should get your eyes checked if cloudy vision, glare or fading colours are starting to affect everyday tasks — reading, cooking, recognising faces or driving. Early on, an updated glasses prescription and better lighting at home can help you manage comfortably. The key is not to wait until your vision has become a real problem; a routine eye test can pick up a cataract long before it seriously limits you.

How Cataracts Are Managed and Treated

In the early stages, many people cope well with stronger glasses, anti-glare lenses and brighter lighting. When a cataract eventually starts to interfere with daily life, an eye specialist may recommend surgery to replace the cloudy natural lens with a clear artificial one. It is one of the most common and well-established eye procedures, but whether and when to have it is a decision only a qualified ophthalmologist can make with you after examining your eyes. Please do not rely on remedies that promise to "dissolve" cataracts without medical advice.

How Cataract Affects Daily Life

Because it develops so gradually, cataract often changes daily habits before anyone connects the dots. You might start switching on more lights to read, holding a book at a different angle, or feeling less confident driving after dark. Bright sunlight may feel harsher than it used to, and colours on a television or in photographs can look a little washed out. Many people quietly give up hobbies like reading, sewing or evening walks without realising a treatable eye change is behind it. Noticing these small shifts early — in yourself or an elderly parent — is exactly what leads to a timely eye test and a clear plan.

Common Myths About Cataract

There is a lot of misinformation about cataract, and some of it can delay people from getting help. A few points worth clearing up:

  • "A cataract is a growth on the eye." It is not. It is a clouding of the eye's own natural lens, not a film or lump growing over the surface.
  • "It only affects very old people." Age is the main factor, but cataract can appear earlier, and health conditions or injury can bring it on sooner.
  • "Eye drops or exercises can dissolve it." There is no reliable home remedy that clears a cataract. Be cautious of products that promise this, and follow professional advice.
  • "You should wait until it is fully ripe." Modern eye care does not require waiting until vision is severely affected. The right timing is a decision to make with an eye specialist based on your daily needs.

Awareness Is the Best First Step

The most powerful thing you can do about cataract is simply to stay aware and get tested regularly. Cloudy vision is not something you have to live with quietly — it can almost always be understood and managed with the right professional care. Common questions about cataract

What is a cataract?

A cataract is a clouding of the eye's natural lens, which sits behind the pupil. As the lens becomes less clear, light struggles to pass through cleanly, so vision gradually turns hazy or blurred. It is very common with age.

What are the early symptoms of cataract?

Common early signs include cloudy or blurry vision, increased glare from lights, halos around headlights at night, faded or yellowed colours, and needing brighter light to read. A proper eye examination is the only way to confirm it.

Can cataracts be prevented?

There is no guaranteed way to prevent cataract, but sensible habits may help: wearing UV-protective sunglasses, not smoking, eating a balanced diet rich in fruit and vegetables, and managing conditions such as diabetes. Regular eye tests help catch changes early.

How are cataracts treated?

In the early stages, updated glasses and better lighting can help you cope. When a cataract begins to affect daily life, an eye specialist may recommend a safe, common surgery to replace the cloudy lens. Only a qualified ophthalmologist can advise on the right timing and treatment for you.
