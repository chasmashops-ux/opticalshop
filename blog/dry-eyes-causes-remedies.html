Dry Eyes: Causes and Simple Remedies

Shree Hari Chasma Ghar 06 May, 2026 6 min read

That gritty, burning feeling — as though there is sand in your eyes — is one of the most common complaints people bring to an optical shop. Dry eye can make screens uncomfortable, reading tiring and even bright light hard to bear. The good news is that for most people it is manageable with a few simple changes. In this article we explain what causes dry eyes, the everyday habits that make them worse, and the practical remedies that genuinely bring relief.

What Are Dry Eyes?

Your eyes are constantly bathed in a thin layer of tears that keeps them smooth, clear and comfortable. This tear film has three parts — an oily layer, a watery layer and a mucous layer — working together to stop tears evaporating too fast. Dry eye happens when you either do not make enough tears, or the tears you make are poor quality and dry out too quickly. The result is a surface that feels irritated, scratchy and tired.

Common Causes of Dry Eyes

Dry eye rarely has a single cause. More often it is a mix of lifestyle and environment. The usual culprits include:

  • Screen time: we blink far less when concentrating on phones and computers.
  • Air conditioning and fans: moving air dries the tear film quickly.
  • Dusty, dry weather: a real factor in Ahmedabad's warm, dusty months.
  • Contact lenses: long hours in lenses can leave eyes feeling parched.
  • Ageing: tear production naturally reduces as we get older.
  • Not drinking enough water: dehydration shows up in your eyes too.

The Screen-Time Connection

If your dry eye flares up during the working week and eases at weekends, screens are almost certainly involved. Normally we blink around 15 to 20 times a minute, but that rate can drop sharply when we stare at a display. Fewer blinks mean the tear film is not refreshed, so it evaporates and leaves the surface exposed. This is why dry eye and digital eye strain so often appear together.

Quick Tip

Try a warm compress. Soak a clean cloth in warm water, wring it out and rest it over closed eyes for five minutes each evening. The gentle heat helps the tiny oil glands along your eyelids work better, which stabilises your tear film and eases that dry, gritty feeling.

Simple Remedies That Help

Most cases of mild dry eye respond well to a few consistent habits. Try these in order and give each a fair chance:

  1. Blink fully and often, especially during screen work.
  2. Follow the 20-20-20 rule — every 20 minutes, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds.
  3. Use lubricating eye drops (artificial tears) for quick relief when needed.
  4. Drink water regularly through the day to stay hydrated.
  5. Position fans and AC vents so they do not blow directly at your face.
  6. Consider a small humidifier in very dry or heavily air-conditioned rooms.

Foods and Everyday Habits

Your overall health shows in your eyes. Eating foods rich in omega-3 fatty acids — such as fish, walnuts and flaxseeds — supports a healthier tear film for some people. Getting enough sleep matters too, because your eyes recover and rehydrate overnight. And if you wear contact lenses, giving your eyes a lens-free day now and then can make a noticeable difference to comfort.

When to See a Professional

Occasional dryness is normal, but you should not ignore symptoms that stick around. Book an eye check if your eyes are constantly dry or painful, if your vision blurs and clears when you blink, if drops stop helping, or if the redness and irritation just will not settle. Persistent dry eye can have underlying reasons that need proper assessment, and self-treating for months is not the answer. A short visit gives you clarity and a proper plan.

Comfortable Eyes, Every Day

Dry eye is common, but it does not have to be something you simply live with. Small, steady changes — more blinking, regular breaks, good hydration and the occasional warm compress — bring real relief for most people. FAQs

Common questions about dry eyes

Why do my eyes feel dry even when they water a lot?

It sounds strange, but watery eyes are a very common sign of dry eye. When the surface of the eye is irritated and lacks a stable tear film, it triggers a flood of reflex tears that drain away quickly without soothing the eye. So watering and dryness often go hand in hand.

Can too much screen time cause dry eyes?

Yes. When we focus on screens we blink far less often, which means the tear film evaporates and is not refreshed. This is one of the most common causes of dry eye today. Taking regular breaks and blinking consciously helps a great deal.

What home remedies help with dry eyes?

Warm compresses, blinking exercises, staying well hydrated, using a humidifier and taking regular screen breaks all help. Lubricating eye drops, often called artificial tears, can give quick relief. If symptoms persist, get your eyes checked rather than relying on drops indefinitely.

When should I see an eye specialist for dry eyes?

See a professional if your dryness is constant, painful, affects your vision, or does not improve with simple remedies over a couple of weeks. Persistent dry eye can have underlying causes that need proper assessment, so it is worth getting a full eye check.
