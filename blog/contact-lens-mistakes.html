Common Contact Lens Mistakes to Avoid

Shree Hari Chasma Ghar 04 Jan, 2026 6 min read

Contact lenses are a wonderful thing — freedom from frames, clear side vision and no fogging in the rain. But because they sit directly on your eye, they demand a little respect. Most contact-related problems are not caused by the lenses themselves; they come from small, everyday shortcuts that quietly put your eyes at risk. The good news is that these mistakes are easy to avoid once you know them. Here are the most common contact lens errors and how to keep your eyes safe and comfortable.

Sleeping in Your Lenses

This is the big one. Unless your lenses are specifically designed and approved for overnight wear, sleeping in them is a genuine hazard. Your cornea needs oxygen, and closing your eyes over a lens for hours starves it while trapping germs against the surface. That combination raises the risk of painful, sometimes serious infections.

Even a quick nap counts. Make it a firm rule: lenses out before bed, every time, unless your optician has specifically told you your lenses are safe for sleeping.

Reusing or Topping Up Solution

It is tempting to simply add a splash of fresh solution to what is already in the case, but this is a real risk. Once used, solution has done its job of disinfecting and loses its power, and it may already be harbouring germs from the previous cycle.

  • Empty the case completely every time.
  • Refill with fresh solution before storing your lenses.
  • Never let the bottle tip touch your fingers, lenses or the case.

Fresh solution, every single time, is one of the cheapest and most effective ways to protect your eyes.

Using Water or Saliva

If a lens feels dry or you drop one, reaching for tap water or, worse, popping it in your mouth to wet it, is a serious mistake. Tap water, bottled water and saliva all carry microbes that have no business near your eyes and can cause stubborn infections.

Only ever use proper contact lens solution to clean, rinse and store your lenses. For the same reason, avoid showering or swimming in your lenses unless you are wearing tight-sealing goggles.

Quick Tip

Always wash and dry your hands thoroughly before touching your lenses, and get into the habit of applying lenses before makeup and removing them before cleansing. This one routine prevents a surprising number of irritations and infections.

Wearing Lenses Too Long

Every lens has a lifespan — daily, fortnightly or monthly — and stretching it is a false economy. Old lenses build up deposits, become less breathable and are far more likely to irritate your eyes or trigger an infection. Squeezing an extra week out of a monthly lens is simply not worth the risk.

The same goes for daily wear time. If your eyes feel tired, dry or red, take the lenses out and give them a rest. Your eyes are telling you something, and glasses make a perfectly good backup.

Neglecting the Lens Case

People obsess over the lenses and forget the case, yet a dirty case can contaminate even a perfectly clean lens. Cases build up a film of germs over time, so they need regular care:

  1. Rinse the case with fresh solution, not water, after each use.
  2. Let it air-dry face down on a clean tissue.
  3. Replace the case every one to three months.
  4. Never reuse a cracked or grimy case.

A clean case is just as important as clean lenses. Treat them as a pair.

Ignoring Warning Signs

Finally, never push through discomfort. Redness, pain, blurred vision, watering or unusual sensitivity to light are signals to stop. Take your lenses out, switch to glasses, and if the symptoms do not settle quickly, get your eyes checked rather than waiting and hoping.

Worn correctly, contact lenses are safe and comfortable for millions of people. It really comes down to clean hands, fresh solution, sticking to the schedule and listening to your eyes. FAQs

Common questions about contact lens safety

Is it really that bad to sleep in my contact lenses?

Yes, unless your lenses are specifically approved for overnight wear. Sleeping in ordinary lenses starves the cornea of oxygen and traps germs against the eye, which raises the risk of serious infection. Always take your lenses out before sleeping unless your optician has told you otherwise.

Can I top up old solution instead of using fresh solution?

No. Topping up used solution is a common and risky habit. Old solution loses its cleaning and disinfecting power and can already be carrying germs. Always empty the case and refill it with fresh solution every single time you store your lenses.

Can I use water or saliva to clean or wet my lenses?

Never. Tap water, bottled water and saliva all contain microbes that should not go near your eyes and can cause serious infections. Only ever use the proper contact lens solution recommended for your lenses to clean, rinse and store them.

How often should I replace my contact lenses and case?

Follow the replacement schedule for your lens type, whether daily, fortnightly or monthly, and never stretch lenses beyond it. Replace your lens case regularly too, ideally every one to three months, since cases build up germs over time even when cleaned.
