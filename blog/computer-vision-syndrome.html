Computer Vision Syndrome: Causes, Symptoms & Relief

Shree Hari Chasma Ghar 28 Jun, 2026 6 min read

By the end of a long working day, do your eyes feel dry, heavy and sore? Does a dull headache creep in around your forehead, and does text on the screen seem to blur every now and then? If so, you are not imagining it. These are the classic signs of Computer Vision Syndrome (also called digital eye strain) — a group of eye and vision problems caused by long, continuous use of screens. It is not a disease and it will not damage your eyes permanently, but it can make your day genuinely uncomfortable. The good news is that most of it is preventable with a few simple changes.

What Is Computer Vision Syndrome?

Computer Vision Syndrome describes the tiredness, discomfort and blurring that build up when your eyes work hard at a screen for hours without a proper break. Screens are demanding in a way that printed paper is not: the text is often lower contrast, the display flickers subtly, there is glare from lights and windows, and we tend to hold phones and laptops closer than is comfortable. Your eyes are forced to focus, refocus and adjust thousands of times an hour, and eventually the tiny muscles that do this work simply get tired.

Why Screens Tire Your Eyes

Several things happen at once when you stare at a display for a long time:

  • You blink far less. We normally blink about 15 times a minute, but this can drop by half at a screen, leaving the surface of the eye dry and gritty.
  • Your focusing muscles stay locked. Holding a fixed near focus for hours keeps these muscles tense, which leads to that tired, strained feeling.
  • Glare and reflections make you squint. Bright windows, overhead tubelights and shiny screens force your eyes to work harder to see clearly.
  • An outdated prescription adds strain. If your glasses are even slightly wrong, your eyes overwork to compensate, and screens make it worse.

Common Symptoms to Watch For

Computer Vision Syndrome shows up differently from person to person, but the most common complaints are:

  • Tired, heavy or aching eyes, especially in the afternoon
  • Headaches around the forehead, temples or behind the eyes
  • Dryness, burning or a gritty, sandy feeling
  • Blurred vision that clears when you look into the distance
  • Difficulty shifting focus between screen and paper
  • Neck, shoulder and upper-back stiffness from poor posture

These symptoms usually ease with rest overnight. If they do not, or if they are getting steadily worse, that is a sign to get your eyes checked.

Quick Tip

Follow the 20-20-20 rule: every 20 minutes, look at something about 20 feet away for at least 20 seconds. Set a gentle reminder on your phone for the first few days until it becomes a habit — it is the single easiest way to give your focusing muscles a rest.

How to Set Up Your Screen the Right Way

A large part of digital eye strain comes down to how your workspace is arranged. Small adjustments make a surprising difference:

  1. Distance: keep your monitor about an arm's length (50–70 cm) from your eyes.
  2. Height: the top of the screen should sit at or just below eye level, so you look slightly downward.
  3. Lighting: position the screen so windows and lights are to the side, not behind or directly in front of it, to avoid reflections.
  4. Brightness: match your screen brightness to the room — it should not glow like a torch in a dim space.
  5. Text size: increase font size so you are not leaning in or squinting to read.

Simple Habits That Bring Relief

Beyond your desk setup, a few everyday habits protect your eyes through long screen days:

  • Blink on purpose. Consciously blink fully now and then to spread the tear film and keep eyes moist.
  • Take real breaks. Stand up, stretch and look out of a window every half hour or so.
  • Stay hydrated. Drinking enough water through the day supports healthy tear production, which matters in a dry, hot city.
  • Use lubricating drops if needed. Simple, preservative-free artificial tears can soothe dryness, but check with a professional if you rely on them daily.
  • Rest your eyes before sleep. Try to step away from bright screens a little while before bed.

Where Glasses and Lenses Help

Sometimes good habits are not enough on their own, and the right eyewear makes all the difference. If your prescription is out of date, updating it can remove strain you did not even realise you were carrying. For heavy screen users, blue cut lenses with an anti-glare coating cut down reflections and make displays more comfortable to look at for long stretches. People over 40 who struggle to focus between screen, keyboard and documents may benefit from lenses designed for intermediate and near distances. The right answer depends on your eyes and your daily routine — which is exactly what a proper eye test uncovers.

Children and Screen Time

It is not only adults who feel the effects. Children now spend long hours on tablets, phones and online classes, and their eyes strain just as much — though they may not describe it clearly. Instead, the signs often show up as behaviour: a child who rubs their eyes frequently, complains of headaches, holds a screen very close, blinks hard or loses concentration during study may well be struggling with digital eye strain.

The same principles that help adults apply to children, with a few extras. Keep screens at a comfortable distance and reduce glare in their study area. Build in regular breaks, and above all encourage plenty of outdoor play — natural daylight and looking into the distance give young eyes exactly the rest they need and support healthy visual development. If your child squints at the television or the classroom board, or seems to tire quickly while reading, a simple eye test is always the right first step.

When to See a Professional

Occasional tired eyes after a marathon work session are normal. But you should book an eye test if the strain is daily, if headaches or blurring continue despite taking breaks, or if it has been more than a year since your last check-up. An optician can rule out an incorrect prescription, dryness or other underlying issues, and recommend lenses suited to your screen life. FAQs

Common questions about Computer Vision Syndrome

What are the first signs of Computer Vision Syndrome?

The earliest signs are usually tired or heavy eyes, a mild headache around the forehead or temples, dryness or a gritty feeling, and slightly blurred vision when you look up from the screen. Symptoms often build up through the day and ease after rest.

Do blue cut lenses help with Computer Vision Syndrome?

They can help. Blue cut lenses with an anti-glare coating reduce reflections and glare from screens, which makes long viewing more comfortable and reduces squinting. They work best alongside good habits like the 20-20-20 rule, correct screen distance and regular blinking.

How does the 20-20-20 rule work?

Every 20 minutes, look at something about 20 feet away for at least 20 seconds. This lets the focusing muscles inside your eyes relax and reminds you to blink fully, which together reduce strain and dryness during long screen sessions.

When should I see an optician about screen-related eye strain?

See an optician if the strain is daily, if headaches or blurring continue despite breaks, or if you have not had an eye test in over a year. An accurate prescription and the right lenses often resolve symptoms that no amount of eye drops can fix.
