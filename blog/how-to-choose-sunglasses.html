How to Choose the Right Sunglasses

Shree Hari Chasma Ghar 02 Feb, 2026 6 min read

Sunglasses are one of those purchases where it is easy to shop with your eyes and forget your eyes' actual needs. A stylish pair catches your attention, you try it on, and you buy — often without a second thought about whether it truly protects you. Under the bright Ahmedabad sun, that protection is not optional. The right sunglasses guard your eyes from harmful rays while looking great; the wrong pair can leave you squinting or, worse, offer no real defence at all. Here is how to choose wisely.

Start With UV Protection

Before you fall for a frame, check one thing above all else: does the lens block ultraviolet light? Prolonged exposure to UV rays is linked to a range of eye problems over the years, so this is the feature that actually protects your health. Look for lenses that block 100 percent of UVA and UVB rays, often labelled UV400.

This is the number one rule, and it is non-negotiable. Everything else — colour, shape, price — comes second to proper UV protection.

Why a Dark Tint Is Not Enough

A common and risky myth is that darker lenses mean more protection. The tint only controls how much visible light reaches your eyes; it says nothing about UV filtering. In fact, dark lenses without a UV filter can be worse than no sunglasses at all — the shade makes your pupils open wider, letting more harmful rays reach the eye.

So never judge sunglasses by how dark they look. Always confirm they are clearly rated for full UV protection, whatever the shade of the lens.

Choosing the Right Lens Colour

Once UV protection is sorted, lens colour is about comfort and how you use your sunglasses. Different tints suit different situations:

  • Grey: a true-to-life view with even glare reduction — a great all-rounder.
  • Brown or amber: boosts contrast, pleasant for driving and bright days.
  • Green: balances contrast and colour accuracy comfortably.
  • Yellow: sharpens contrast in low light, popular for overcast conditions.

There is no single best colour — it comes down to where and how you will wear them most.

Quick Tip

If you drive a lot or spend time near water, consider polarized lenses. They cut the blinding glare bouncing off roads, bonnets and water surfaces, making bright-day driving noticeably more comfortable and less tiring on your eyes.

Getting the Fit Right

Even the best lenses fail if the sunglasses do not fit. Gaps around the frame let stray light and UV rays sneak in from the sides, so coverage matters. A good fit should feel secure without pinching, and the frame should not slide down when you look down or move around.

Larger and wrap-around styles give more coverage, which is handy for driving, sport and long hours outdoors. Whatever the shape, make sure the arms sit comfortably over your ears and the bridge rests gently on your nose.

Matching Sunglasses to Your Face

Style is where sunglasses become fun, and the classic trick is to balance your face shape with a contrasting frame. Use this as a loose guide rather than a strict rule:

  1. Round face: angular or rectangular frames add definition.
  2. Square face: round or oval frames soften strong angles.
  3. Oval face: most shapes suit you, so wear what you love.
  4. Heart-shaped face: lighter, bottom-heavy frames balance a wider forehead.

The best pair is one that protects your eyes and makes you feel confident every time you put it on.

Prescription and Everyday Options

If you wear glasses, you do not have to choose between clear vision and sun protection. Prescription sunglasses give you both, and photochromic lenses that darken automatically in sunlight are a convenient all-in-one option for people who move in and out of the sun through the day.

Whether you want a simple everyday pair, driving sunglasses or a prescription option, it helps to try a few on and see what feels right. FAQs

Common questions about choosing sunglasses

What is the most important feature when buying sunglasses?

UV protection is the single most important feature. Look for sunglasses that block 100 percent of UVA and UVB rays, often labelled UV400. Style and colour matter, but a dark tint without proper UV protection can actually do more harm than good.

Do darker lenses mean better sun protection?

No. The darkness of the tint is about comfort and glare, not UV protection. A very dark lens without a proper UV filter lets your pupils open wider and allows more harmful rays in. Always choose lenses that are clearly rated for full UV protection, whatever the tint.

Are polarized sunglasses worth it?

For many people, yes. Polarized lenses cut harsh glare from reflective surfaces like roads, water and car bonnets, which makes driving and outdoor activities more comfortable. They are especially useful in bright conditions, though they are a comfort feature rather than a substitute for UV protection.

Can I get sunglasses with my prescription?

Yes. Prescription sunglasses let you see clearly and protect your eyes at the same time, so you do not have to choose between vision and sun protection. Photochromic lenses that darken in sunlight are another convenient option worth asking your optician about.
