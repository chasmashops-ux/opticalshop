How Often Should You Get an Eye Test?

Shree Hari Chasma Ghar 28 Apr, 2026 6 min read

Most of us only think about our eyes when something goes wrong — a headache after a long day, a road sign that will not sharpen, small print that suddenly looks smaller. But an eye test is not only for when you notice trouble. It is a health check for one of your most important senses, and a chance to catch quiet changes early. So how often should you actually go? The honest answer depends on your age, your health and your daily habits. Here is a clear, practical guide.

Why Regular Eye Tests Matter

An eye test does two jobs at once. First, it checks whether your vision is sharp and whether your prescription has changed. Second — and this is the part people forget — it examines the health of your eyes. Many conditions develop slowly and painlessly, giving no early warning you would notice on your own. A routine check can flag the first signs long before they affect your sight.

Your eyes can also reveal clues about your wider health. During an examination, an optician can sometimes spot signs linked to conditions such as high blood pressure or diabetes. In other words, a good eye test is far more than a quick read of the letter chart.

General Guidance by Age Group

There is no single rule that fits everyone, but these ranges work well for most people with no known eye problems:

  • Children: a check before starting school, then every one to two years, or sooner if you spot any concern.
  • Adults (roughly 18–60): every one to two years for most people.
  • Older adults (60+): yearly, since the risk of age-related conditions rises with age.

These are general guidelines. Your optician may suggest checking more or less often based on your prescription, family history and overall health.

When You Should Go More Often

Some people benefit from more frequent tests. Consider shorter gaps between checks if any of the following apply to you:

  1. You wear glasses or contact lenses with a changing prescription.
  2. You have a family history of eye conditions.
  3. You live with diabetes or high blood pressure.
  4. You spend long hours on screens every day.
  5. You have had eye surgery or an eye injury in the past.

If you fall into one of these groups, your optician will recommend a schedule that keeps a closer eye on any changes.

Quick Tip

Do not wait for your vision to blur before booking. Tie your eye test to an easy annual anchor — a birthday or the start of a new school year — so it becomes a habit you never forget rather than something you keep putting off.

Signs You Should Book Sooner

Whatever your usual schedule, some symptoms mean you should not wait for the next routine visit. Book an appointment promptly if you notice:

  • Blurry or double vision that comes and goes.
  • Frequent headaches or eye strain, especially after reading or screens.
  • Difficulty seeing at night or extra sensitivity to glare.
  • Sudden flashes, floaters or a shadow across your vision.
  • Eyes that feel constantly dry, red, watery or tired.

Sudden or severe changes in vision should be treated as urgent and checked without delay. When in doubt, it is always better to have your eyes looked at than to wait and wonder.

What Happens During an Eye Test

If it has been a while, the process is simpler and more comfortable than you might expect. A typical visit includes reading a letter chart to measure your sharpness, checking your current prescription, and examining the front and back of your eyes. The whole thing is painless and usually takes well under an hour.

You will leave knowing exactly where your vision stands — and if you do need glasses or a new prescription, you can sort it out in the same visit. There is real peace of mind in simply knowing your eyes are healthy.

Make It a Habit

Clear, comfortable vision is easy to take for granted until it slips. Regular eye tests are one of the simplest ways to protect your sight for the long term, whatever your age. If it has been more than a year or two since your last check, treat this as your reminder.

FAQs

Common questions about eye test frequency

How often should a healthy adult get an eye test?

For most adults with no known problems, an eye test every one to two years is a sensible routine. It checks not just your vision but the overall health of your eyes, and it catches gradual changes you may not notice on your own.

Do I still need an eye test if my vision seems perfect?

Yes. Many eye conditions develop slowly and painlessly, without any obvious change in your sight until later. A routine eye test can pick up early signs long before you would notice a problem yourself, which is exactly why regular checks matter.

How often should children have their eyes checked?

Children should have an eye test before starting school and then roughly every one to two years, or sooner if you notice any warning signs. Because children rarely report vision trouble, regular checks are the best way to catch and treat issues early.

Should older adults get their eyes tested more often?

Yes. From around the age of 60, checking your eyes every year is wise, as the risk of age-related conditions rises. Your optician can advise a schedule that suits your health, and you should always book sooner if you notice new changes in your vision.
