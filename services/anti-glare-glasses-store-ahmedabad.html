What Anti Glare Coating Actually Does

A plain lens reflects light off both surfaces — that's the faint glare you sometimes see in photos of someone wearing glasses. Anti glare (anti-reflective) coating cuts that reflection down, so more light passes through the lens instead of bouncing around. The practical result: screens look clearer, oncoming headlights scatter less at night, and people looking at you can actually see your eyes instead of a glare patch.

We see this requested most often by people who work on laptops all day, students studying late under tube lights, and anyone who drives at night and finds headlight glare uncomfortable. It's a coating, not a power change — your prescription stays exactly the same, we just finish the lens differently.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I add anti glare coating to glasses I already own?

Only if we're also replacing the lenses — coating is applied during lens manufacturing, not added afterward to an existing lens. If your frame is still in good shape, we can fit new coated lenses into it.

Does anti glare coating wear off over time?

With normal use and proper cleaning (microfiber cloth, no dry wiping with cloth or tissue), a quality coating lasts the life of the lens. Cheap coatings can peel at the edges after a year or two — we use coatings rated for durability, not the lowest-cost option.

Is anti glare coating worth it for everyday glasses, not just computer use?

Most customers who try it don't go back to uncoated lenses — it helps with driving, indoor lighting and screens alike, so unless budget is tight we generally recommend it as a default rather than an extra.

How much extra does it cost over regular lenses?

It depends on your lens type and power, so the fairest way to answer is to quote it alongside your prescription when you visit — call us and we can give you a rough number over the phone first.
